Part I: The Diseases

1. What Is Alpha-Synuclein & How It Causes Disease

The Normal Protein

Alpha-synuclein is a small protein (140 amino acids) found abundantly at the tips of nerve cells (presynaptic terminals) throughout the brain. In its healthy state, it is a flexible, unstructured protein — like a loose piece of string. Its normal job is to help regulate the release of neurotransmitters, particularly dopamine, by interacting with the membranes of synaptic vesicles (the tiny packages that carry chemical signals between neurons).

Think of it this way: neurons communicate by releasing chemical messengers across tiny gaps (synapses). Alpha-synuclein helps manage the packaging and delivery of these messengers. It's a traffic controller at the neural communication junction.

When It Goes Wrong: Misfolding and Aggregation

Under certain conditions — which can include genetic mutations, aging, oxidative stress, or environmental toxins — alpha-synuclein changes shape. Instead of staying loose and flexible, it folds into rigid, stacked structures called beta-sheets. These misfolded copies stick together like Velcro, first forming small clumps (oligomers), then longer chains (fibrils), and eventually large, dense deposits visible under a microscope.

What Are Lewy Bodies?

When enough misfolded alpha-synuclein accumulates inside a neuron, it forms a large, round inclusion called a Lewy body. Lewy bodies are the pathological hallmark of Parkinson's disease and Dementia with Lewy Bodies (DLB). They are like garbage dumps inside the cell — the neuron's waste disposal system has been overwhelmed.

Neurons containing Lewy bodies are sick and dysfunctional. Eventually, they die. When the dying neurons are in the substantia nigra (the brain's dopamine factory), the result is the motor symptoms of Parkinson's: tremor, stiffness, slowness of movement.

Prion-Like Spread

One of the most important discoveries of the last decade: misfolded alpha-synuclein can spread from cell to cell, like a contagion. A misfolded copy can be released from one neuron, taken up by a neighboring neuron, and then cause the normal alpha-synuclein in that neuron to misfold too. This is called "prion-like" templating — the bad copy serves as a template, forcing good copies to adopt its abnormal shape.

The mechanism works like this: (1) Misfolded fibrils in one neuron act as seeds. (2) Seeds are released through exosomes (small membrane vesicles), direct secretion, or when the neuron dies. (3) Neighboring or connected neurons take up the seeds via endocytosis. (4) Inside the new neuron, the seed recruits and converts normal alpha-synuclein into the misfolded form — one seed can convert many normal molecules. (5) Critically, spread follows neuronal connectivity, not simple proximity — it travels along axons from one connected brain region to the next.

The strongest evidence came from studies of patients who received fetal dopaminergic neuron transplants — years later, the healthy transplanted neurons had developed Lewy body pathology, proving misfolded alpha-synuclein had spread from the host's diseased neurons into the grafted ones.

Braak Staging: The Predictable Path of Spread

Alpha-synuclein pathology spreads through the brain in a stereotyped, predictable pattern described by the Braak staging system:

StageBrain Regions AffectedClinical Correlation
1-2Olfactory bulb, dorsal motor nucleus of vagus (brainstem)Loss of smell, constipation — years BEFORE motor symptoms
3Substantia nigra, other midbrain/pontine nucleiMotor symptoms appear (tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia) — typically after 50-70% of dopamine neurons are already lost
4Temporal mesocortex, amygdalaMood changes, anxiety, mild cognitive effects
5-6Neocortex (widespread)Cognitive decline, dementia (PDD/DLB)

There are two proposed propagation patterns:

  • Body-first (bottom-up): Misfolding starts in the gut nervous system or peripheral nerves, travels up the vagus nerve to the brainstem, then to the midbrain and cortex. These patients often have early constipation, REM sleep behavior disorder, and reduced sense of smell BEFORE motor symptoms.
  • Brain-first (top-down): Misfolding starts in the brain itself (amygdala or cortex), then spreads down. These patients may develop motor symptoms without the early autonomic or sleep disturbances.
What the positive skin biopsy means: The skin biopsy found phosphorylated alpha-synuclein in peripheral nerve fibers. This confirms that the misfolded protein is present OUTSIDE the brain — in the peripheral nervous system. This is consistent with the synucleinopathy diagnosis and suggests the disease involves the body's nerve network, not just the brain. The Synuclein-One skin biopsy has 92.7% sensitivity for detecting PD (JAMA 2024).

2. What Is Tau & Why PSP Is Different from Alzheimer's

The Normal Protein

Tau is a protein that stabilizes microtubules — the internal scaffolding of neurons. Think of microtubules as the railroad tracks inside a nerve cell: they provide structure and serve as highways for transporting nutrients and signals from the cell body all the way down long axons to distant synapses. Tau proteins are the railroad ties that hold these tracks together and in proper shape.

Tau Isoforms: 3R vs. 4R

The human brain produces six different versions (isoforms) of tau, which differ in how many "repeat" domains they have for binding to microtubules. The two important categories are:

  • 3-repeat tau (3R): Has three microtubule-binding domains. More common in fetal brain.
  • 4-repeat tau (4R): Has four microtubule-binding domains. Binds microtubules more tightly.

In a healthy adult brain, the ratio of 3R to 4R tau is roughly 1:1. Different diseases disturb this balance in different ways — and this distinction is critical for diagnosis and treatment:

DiseaseTau TypeImplications
Alzheimer's DiseaseMixed 3R + 4RBoth isoforms aggregate together
PSP (all variants)4R onlyOnly 4R tau aggregates; requires different treatments and imaging tracers
CBD (Corticobasal Degeneration)4R onlySimilar to PSP; different brain distribution
Pick's Disease3R onlyDifferent from both PSP and AD
Critical implication for this patient: PSP is a 4R-only tauopathy. This means the tau PET imaging tracer matters enormously. The commonly used tracer flortaucipir (AV-1451) was designed for Alzheimer's mixed 3R/4R tau and binds POORLY to 4R-only tau deposits. For PSP, newer tracers like PI-2620 (85% sensitivity for PSP-RS) or florzolotau/PM-PBB3 (AUC 0.87) should be used instead. Using the wrong tracer could produce a false negative.

How Tau Becomes Pathological

When tau is hyperphosphorylated (too many phosphate groups are attached to it by enzymes called kinases), it loses its ability to bind microtubules. The detached, hyperphosphorylated tau then misfolds and aggregates, forming neurofibrillary tangles (NFTs). These tangles clog the neuron's interior, disrupting transport and eventually killing the cell.

The key phosphorylation sites that researchers measure as biomarkers:

  • p-tau 181: An early phosphorylation event; elevated in Alzheimer's but may be lower in PSP (useful for differential diagnosis)
  • p-tau 217: The most specific marker for tau aggregation; primarily reflects Alzheimer's-type pathology, so LOW p-tau 217 with HIGH neurofilament light is a pattern that SUPPORTS a 4R-tauopathy diagnosis (PSP)
  • p-tau 231: Reflects an early stage of tau pathology; may change before clinical symptoms

How Tau Spreads

Like alpha-synuclein, tau can spread from neuron to neuron in a prion-like fashion. Misfolded tau is released from one cell, taken up by another, and templates the misfolding of normal tau in the recipient cell. In PSP, the spread follows a distinctive pattern affecting subcortical structures (basal ganglia, brainstem, cerebellum) rather than the cortical pattern seen in Alzheimer's.

3. Cross-Seeding: How the Two Proteins Interact

This section describes the single most important concept for understanding this patient's disease.

What Is Cross-Seeding?

Cross-seeding is the process by which one misfolded protein promotes the misfolding of a different protein. In this patient's case:

  • Misfolded alpha-synuclein fibrils can serve as templates that cause normal tau to misfold
  • Misfolded tau can likewise promote alpha-synuclein misfolding
  • The process is bidirectional — each protein accelerates the other's pathology
Alpha-Synuclein Tau (confirmed ✓) (suspected, PSP features) ═══╗ ╔═══ ╚═══ ──── cross-seeds ───→ ═══╗ ═══╗ ←─── cross-seeds ──── ╚═══ ╚═══ ═══╗ Result: ACCELERATED neurodegeneration when both proteins are present BUT ALSO: Therapies targeting EITHER protein may reduce BOTH via the same cross-seeding mechanism in reverse

The Science Behind Cross-Seeding

A landmark 2025 Frontiers in Neuroscience review established that synucleinopathies should be redefined as "complex proteinopathies" because they almost always involve multiple misfolded proteins. Key findings:

  • Alpha-synuclein fibrils template tau misfolding: When alpha-synuclein aggregates form in a neuron, they create a cellular environment that promotes tau hyperphosphorylation and aggregation. The mechanisms include: disrupting kinase/phosphatase balance (more tau phosphorylation), impairing protein degradation pathways (less tau clearance), and direct physical interaction between the two proteins.
  • Tau oligomers promote alpha-synuclein aggregation: Tau oligomers (the small, toxic clumps before tangles form) can directly interact with alpha-synuclein and accelerate its aggregation.
  • Co-aggregation is synergistic: When both proteins are present and misfolding, the neuronal dysfunction is GREATER than what either protein alone would cause. This is not additive — it's multiplicative.
The TOMA Discovery — Why This Matters for Treatment: Tau Oligomer-specific Monoclonal Antibodies (TOMAs) were developed to target only the toxic tau oligomers (not the normal, functional tau or the inert tangles). In mouse models, these antibodies decreased not only tau oligomers but also insoluble alpha-synuclein oligomers. This is profound: targeting tau reduced alpha-synuclein pathology too. It means the cross-seeding mechanism is a therapeutic LEVER — reduce one protein's toxicity, and the other's toxicity decreases as a secondary benefit.

Implications for This Patient

The dual proteinopathy is not a worst-case scenario — it is actually an opportunity:

  1. More targets = more options. A patient with only alpha-synuclein pathology can only benefit from alpha-synuclein therapies. A patient with both pathologies can benefit from alpha-synuclein therapies AND tau therapies AND cross-seeding interventions.
  2. Cross-seeding means dual benefit. An effective alpha-synuclein vaccine may also reduce tau pathology (and vice versa), because disrupting the cross-seeding cycle at either point should slow both processes.
  3. More trial eligibility. This patient may qualify for BOTH alpha-synuclein trials (prasinezumab, VacSYn) AND tau trials (AADvac1 PSP Platform Trial).

4. PSP-Richardson's Syndrome: The Clinical Picture

What Is PSP?

Progressive Supranuclear Palsy (PSP) is a neurodegenerative disease caused by the accumulation of 4-repeat tau in specific brain regions: the basal ganglia, brainstem, and cerebellum. It is the most common atypical parkinsonian disorder, but still rare — affecting about 6 per 100,000 people.

"Supranuclear" refers to the location of the damage: ABOVE (supra) the cranial nerve NUCLEI in the brainstem. The nuclei that directly control eye muscles still work — but the brain regions that COMMAND those nuclei to move are damaged. This is why PSP patients can't voluntarily look up or down, but their eyes still move reflexively (for example, the doll's eye maneuver, where the doctor turns the patient's head, still produces eye movement).

PSP-Richardson's vs. Other PSP Variants

PSP is not a single disease — it comes in several clinical variants. PSP-Richardson's syndrome (PSP-RS) is the "classic" and most common form:

The Eye Movement Problem Explained

Supranuclear gaze palsy is the hallmark of PSP-RS. Here's what happens:

  1. Vertical saccades slow first: The quick, darting eye movements (saccades) that we use to look up and down become slow. The patient is asked to look at the examiner's finger above, then below — the eyes move sluggishly.
  2. Downward gaze affected next: The inability to look down is particularly disabling. Patients trip on curbs, can't see their food, and have trouble reading.
  3. Eventually, voluntary gaze is lost: The eyes become "locked" in midposition. Importantly, reflexive eye movements (e.g., rotating the head) still work because the lower brainstem nuclei are intact.

The brain regions damaged are the rostral interstitial nucleus of the medial longitudinal fasciculus (riMLF) and the interstitial nucleus of Cajal — brainstem structures that control vertical gaze commands.

Why Backward Falls Happen

The retropulsive (backward) falls in PSP result from damage to multiple systems simultaneously:

  • Postural instability: Damage to the pedunculopontine nucleus (a brainstem locomotor center) disrupts the ability to make corrective postural adjustments.
  • Axial rigidity: Stiffness in the trunk muscles (more than the limbs) prevents the normal compensatory bending and swaying that keeps us upright.
  • Impaired visual feedback: With eye movement problems, the patient can't quickly scan the environment to orient themselves.
  • Frontal lobe dysfunction: Impaired judgment about risk (stepping backward without checking, rising from a chair too quickly).

Important nuance for this patient: This patient has features of BOTH PD and PSP-RS. The levodopa-responsive tremor is a PD feature, while the backward falls and eye movement abnormalities are PSP features. This overlap is consistent with a dual proteinopathy where both alpha-synuclein and tau are contributing to the clinical picture. The progression timeline may not follow a "pure" PSP-RS course — it could be slower or different in pattern.

8. Priority Tier A — Critical Tests (Do Now)

These tests should be discussed with the neurologist at the earliest opportunity. Each provides information that is either safety-critical, prognostically important, or required for clinical trial enrollment.

A1. Videofluoroscopic Swallow Study (VFSS)

SAFETY PRIORITY: Aspiration pneumonia is the leading cause of death in PSP. Dysphagia (swallowing difficulty) occurs in 78-89% of PSP patients and can be SILENT — patients may not realize they are aspirating. A baseline swallowing assessment can identify risk early and guide dietary modifications that are genuinely life-prolonging.
AspectDetails
What it isThe patient swallows barium-coated foods and liquids of different consistencies while being imaged by fluoroscopy (real-time X-ray). Also called a modified barium swallow study.
What it revealsOral phase problems (food pooling in the mouth, difficulty initiating swallow), pharyngeal residue (food remaining after swallow), silent aspiration (food/liquid entering the airway without coughing), swallow timing and coordination.
Why it matters in PSPPSP dysphagia has a distinctive pattern: oral phase predominance (98% of studies show oral residue; 77% show bolus transfer difficulties), pharyngeal residue (70%), and delayed swallow initiation (68%). Even if the patient does not report swallowing difficulty, silent aspiration is common.
Where to get itSpeech-language pathology department at any hospital. Outpatient procedure. Often can be bundled with speech assessment.
EvidenceTier 1 Systematic review and meta-analysis (2024); scoping review (December 2024).
ActionRequest at next neurology visit: "Given the PSP diagnosis, can we get a baseline videofluoroscopic swallow study to assess aspiration risk?"

A2. Blood-Based Biomarker Panel

A single blood draw can now provide three powerful biomarkers. This is one of the highest-value, lowest-burden tests available.

BiomarkerWhat It MeasuresWhat It Means for This Patient
Neurofilament Light Chain (NfL) A structural protein released when neurons are damaged. Think of it as a "neuronal damage meter" — more damage = higher NfL. NfL is the strongest prognostic biomarker in PSP. Levels run ~2x healthy controls. Higher levels predict worse motor/cognitive outcomes AND shorter survival. It's the closest thing to a "speedometer" showing how fast the disease is progressing. It also differentiates PSP from PD (PSP has higher NfL).
Plasma GFAP Glial Fibrillary Acidic Protein — a marker of astrocyte activation (brain inflammation). Astrocytes are the brain's support cells; when neurons are dying, astrocytes become reactive. Predicts cognitive decline and conversion to dementia (AUC 0.90 for MCI-to-dementia conversion). Given PSP involves frontal cognitive dysfunction, GFAP provides a baseline for tracking this dimension. Also helps characterize the inflammatory component of the disease.
Plasma p-tau 217 Phosphorylated tau at position 217. The most specific blood-based marker for tau aggregation. Here's the nuance: p-tau 217 primarily reflects Alzheimer's-type (mixed 3R/4R) tau, NOT the 4R-only tau found in PSP. So for this patient, a LOW p-tau 217 with HIGH NfL actually SUPPORTS the PSP diagnosis (4R-tauopathy, not Alzheimer's). A high p-tau 217 would suggest Alzheimer's co-pathology, which would change the clinical picture.
Where to get it: Available through Quest Diagnostics (NfL), C2N Diagnostics (PrecivityAD2 for p-tau217), Quanterix-partnered labs. Major academic centers can order all three. Request at next neurology visit: "Can we get baseline plasma neurofilament light, GFAP, and p-tau 217 levels? These would help track disease progression and differentiate the tau phenotype."
Tier 1 Multiple large cohort studies, including Neurology December 2025 (275 participants).

B2. Speech and Language Assessment

Dysarthria (slurred speech) occurs in the majority of PSP patients, with hypokinetic dysarthria being most common (52%). Apraxia of speech co-occurs in 19%. A speech assessment complements the swallowing assessment because the same brainstem circuits control both functions.

Can typically be done at the same visit as the swallowing study (same department). Request the Apraxia of Speech Rating Scale (ASRS-3) and Motor Speech Disorders Severity Rating Scale.

B3. Olfactory Testing

A simple scratch-and-sniff test (UPSIT, 40 items, 15 minutes) that helps differentiate PD from PSP:

  • PD: Causes severe olfactory loss (hyposmia/anosmia)
  • PSP: Causes moderate, less severe olfactory loss
  • A 2025 simplified 7-item test distinguishes PD/DLB from PSP/MSA with AUC 0.73

For a patient with both PD and PSP features, severe loss favors synuclein-dominant pathology; moderate loss favors tau-dominant.

B4. Autonomic Function Assessment

Start with the COMPASS-31 questionnaire (31 questions, self-administered, validated in parkinsonism with 92.6% sensitivity for autonomic dysfunction). Covers: orthostatic intolerance, vasomotor, secretomotor, gastrointestinal, bladder, and pupillomotor domains.

If the questionnaire shows significant autonomic dysfunction, formal testing (QSART, heart rate variability, tilt table test) can be done at an autonomic lab (Mayo Clinic is the gold standard; Cleveland Clinic, Beth Israel, Pitt also have excellent labs).

Why this matters for falls: The patient's backward falls may be purely retropulsive (postural instability from PSP) OR may have an orthostatic hypotension component (from synuclein-driven autonomic dysfunction). Orthostatic hypotension is TREATABLE (midodrine, compression stockings, hydration, salt supplementation). If autonomic testing reveals an orthostatic component, addressing it could reduce falls immediately.

B5. Retinal OCT (Optical Coherence Tomography)

Non-invasive retinal imaging (done at any ophthalmology office, takes 5 minutes) that can differentiate PD from PSP:

  • PD: Shows parafoveal ganglion cell-inner plexiform layer (GCIPL) thinning
  • PSP: Shows BOTH GCIPL AND outer nuclear layer (ONL) reduction — more extensive retinal involvement

The retina is essentially exposed brain tissue, providing a non-invasive window into neurodegeneration. Quick, painless, and provides baseline for longitudinal tracking.

10. Priority Tier C — Supplementary Tests

C1. REM Sleep Behavior Disorder Screening

Ask about acting out dreams during sleep (punching, kicking, yelling). Present in >90% of prodromal synucleinopathies. Since alpha-synuclein is already confirmed, this adds safety information (injury risk during sleep) rather than diagnostic value. Use the RBD Screening Questionnaire (single page, validated).

C2. Digital/Wearable Monitoring

An Apple Watch with fall detection provides immediate safety value. Research-grade inertial measurement units can continuously monitor gait speed, stride length, arm swing asymmetry, and fall patterns — but standardized clinical protocols don't exist yet.

11. How Each Test Works (Deep Dives)

Skin Biopsy for Alpha-Synuclein (Already Done)

The Synuclein-One test takes 3mm punch biopsies from specific body sites (typically posterior cervical region, above the knee, and above the ankle). The tissue is stained with antibodies that specifically bind to phosphorylated alpha-synuclein (p-Ser129). Positive staining means misfolded, phosphorylated alpha-synuclein is present in the cutaneous nerve fibers.

The test detects a peripheral manifestation of a central nervous system disease. Alpha-synuclein pathology extends beyond the brain into the peripheral nervous system via the autonomic nerves. This is why the skin biopsy works — the peripheral nerves running through skin are part of the same nervous system being attacked by the disease.

Seed Amplification Assay (SAA / RT-QuIC)

This test exploits the prion-like property of alpha-synuclein. Here's how it works:

  1. A CSF sample is placed in a well with a large excess of normal, recombinant alpha-synuclein protein
  2. A fluorescent dye (Thioflavin T) is added that only fluoresces when bound to fibrillar (misfolded) protein
  3. The plate is subjected to cycles of shaking and resting at 37°C
  4. If the CSF contains even tiny amounts of misfolded alpha-synuclein "seeds," they will recruit the normal protein to misfold, forming fibrils
  5. The fluorescence signal increases exponentially as fibrils form
  6. The speed of this amplification (kinetics) correlates with disease characteristics

Faster seeding kinetics are associated with more aggressive disease and predict cognitive decline, especially in patients with GBA1 variants (Lancet Neurology 2025).

DaT-SPECT (Dopamine Transporter Scan)

Dopamine-producing neurons have transporter proteins on their surface that recycle dopamine from the synapse. When these neurons die (as in PD and PSP), the transporter density decreases. The DaTscan radiotracer binds to these transporters, producing an image of surviving dopamine neurons.

  • Normal scan: Two comma-shaped regions of bright uptake in the striatum (caudate + putamen) — looks like two commas or a pair of quotation marks
  • Abnormal scan (PD): The "tail" of the comma (putamen) loses signal first, leaving just dots (caudate only) — called "period sign" or "egg-shaped" pattern
  • Abnormal scan (PSP): May show more symmetric loss than PD

Tau PET Imaging (PI-2620)

The PI-2620 radiotracer is injected intravenously. Over 60-90 minutes, it crosses the blood-brain barrier and binds to misfolded tau aggregates in the brain. PET scanning then produces a 3D map of tau deposition.

In PSP, the characteristic pattern shows uptake in:

  • Globus pallidus (part of basal ganglia)
  • Subthalamic nucleus
  • Midbrain (substantia nigra, red nucleus, periaqueductal gray)
  • Pons and cerebellum (dentate nucleus)

This subcortical pattern is distinctly different from Alzheimer's, where tau PET shows cortical uptake (temporal and parietal lobes).

Cardiac MIBG Scan

MIBG (meta-iodobenzylguanidine) is a norepinephrine analog. Cardiac sympathetic nerve endings take it up just like they take up norepinephrine. In PD, alpha-synuclein deposits damage these nerve endings early, so they can't take up MIBG properly — resulting in a low heart-to-mediastinum (H/M) ratio. In PSP, these nerves are preserved (normal H/M ratio).

Part III: Personalized Genomic Medicine

This section describes the 3-phase roadmap for moving from generic treatment landscape to personalized therapy selection based on the patient's own genetic and immune profile. This is the most novel and forward-looking aspect of the research program.

12. Phase 1: Getting the Genetic Data

Current Status: Referral to genetics specialist has been requested through the neurologist. This phase is the foundation — everything in Phases 2 and 3 depends on obtaining genetic data.

Step 1a: Genetic Counseling Referral

A genetic counselor specializing in neurodegeneration will explain what tests are available, what results might mean, help navigate insurance coverage, and provide emotional support for genetic findings that may affect other family members.

What to say: "I'd like a referral to a genetic counselor to discuss comprehensive genetic testing. We want to identify any variants that could affect treatment selection or qualify for gene-targeted clinical trials."

Step 1b: Invitae PD Panel (Clinical-Grade)

The Invitae Parkinson's Disease genetic panel covers the major PD-associated genes: GBA, LRRK2, SNCA, PARK2/Parkin, PINK1, DJ-1, VPS35, and others. For patients with a PD diagnosis, copay is often $0 through their patient assistance program. Results arrive in 2-3 weeks as a clinical report with variant interpretation.

Step 1c: Whole Genome Sequencing (GP2 or Commercial)

The Global Parkinson's Genetics Program (GP2) offers free whole genome sequencing to PD patients at participating sites. WGS provides far more data than a panel test — it captures the entire genome, enabling all computational analyses including HLA typing, polygenic risk scoring, and discovery of novel variants.

Alternative commercial WGS: Nebula Genomics (~$299) or Dante Labs (~$199) for 30x coverage.

Step 1d: HLA Typing

HLA (Human Leukocyte Antigen) molecules determine which peptide fragments the immune system can "present" to T cells. This is critical for predicting vaccine response:

  • Each person has a unique set of HLA molecules (inherited from both parents)
  • Each HLA molecule can only present peptides of certain shapes and chemical properties
  • Vaccines work by presenting specific peptide epitopes to the immune system via HLA molecules
  • If the patient's HLA molecules can't bind the vaccine's peptide epitopes, the vaccine may not generate a strong immune response

HLA type can be extracted computationally from WGS/WES data, or ordered separately via clinical labs.

13. Phase 2: Computational Analysis

Once genetic data is obtained, four computational analyses become possible. These pipelines will be built on the DR3 dashboard:

Step 2a: Variant Interpretation Pipeline

Upload the VCF (Variant Call Format) file. Filter for neurodegeneration-relevant genes. Annotate each variant with ClinVar pathogenicity classification, gnomAD population frequency, CADD score (predicted deleteriousness), and protein impact prediction (SIFT, PolyPhen-2).

Output: Prioritized list of clinically relevant variants with drug interaction annotations.

Step 2b: Drug-Gene Interaction Screening

Cross-reference the patient's variants against the Drug-Gene Interaction Database (DGIdb), PharmGKB, and ClinicalTrials.gov to identify: FDA-approved drugs targeting the patient's variant pathways (repurposing candidates), clinical trials recruiting for specific genotypes, and pharmacogenomic interactions affecting drug metabolism.

Step 2c: HLA-Epitope Vaccine Compatibility Prediction

This is the novel analysis. Using NetMHCpan 4.1 or MHCflurry 2.0, predict how strongly the patient's specific HLA molecules will bind to the peptide epitopes used in each trial vaccine:

  • VacSYn (ACI-7104.056) — alpha-synuclein oligomer-mimicking peptide
  • UB-312 — C-terminal alpha-synuclein peptide
  • AADvac1 — tau peptide (mis-disordered tau region)
  • Anti-pT181 mucosal vaccine — phospho-tau 181 epitope

Output: A vaccine compatibility matrix showing predicted binding affinity (IC50) for each vaccine × HLA combination. This tells us which vaccines the patient's immune system is best equipped to respond to.

Why this is novel: HLA-epitope prediction is standard in cancer immunotherapy (it's how personalized cancer vaccines are designed). Applying it to neurodegenerative disease vaccines is a new idea. It hasn't been done clinically because (a) the vaccines are new, and (b) nobody has connected the oncology immunoinformatics tools to the PD/PSP field. We are bridging that gap computationally.

Step 2d: Structural Variant Modeling (AlphaFold3)

If the patient has any missense variants in SNCA, MAPT, or GBA, predict how these variants affect protein structure using AlphaFold3. Overlay with known drug binding sites and vaccine epitope locations. Visualize using interactive 3D protein viewer on the dashboard.

14. Phase 3: Personalized Treatment Ranking

Step 3a: Re-rank All Therapies by Patient-Specific Relevance

Using genetic data + HLA compatibility + drug-gene interactions, compute a composite score for every tracked therapy. A therapy targeting a pathway affected by the patient's specific variants ranks higher. A vaccine whose epitopes bind strongly to the patient's HLA ranks higher. Display as a personalized therapy ranking, replacing the generic evidence-tier sort.

Step 3b: Genetic Trial Eligibility Report

Many trials have genetic inclusion/exclusion criteria. With genetic data, automatically filter to show only trials the patient is genetically eligible for.

Step 3c: Neurologist Discussion

Bring the personalized report to the neurologist. Walk through genetic variants found, trial eligibility, vaccine compatibility predictions, and drug repurposing candidates. The computational findings INFORM the clinical conversation; they do not replace it.

Part IV: The Treatment Landscape

15. How Immunotherapy Works in Neurodegeneration

The Basic Problem

Misfolded alpha-synuclein and tau proteins accumulate in the brain. The brain's own immune cells (microglia) try to clear them but are overwhelmed. The proteins spread faster than the brain can remove them. Immunotherapy aims to tip the balance by providing the immune system with targeted weapons.

Active Immunotherapy (Vaccines)

A vaccine presents a piece of the target protein (an "epitope") to the immune system. Here is what happens step by step after a vaccine like ACI-7104.056 is injected:

  1. Injection: The vaccine peptide (mimicking oligomerized alpha-synuclein), conjugated to a carrier protein and mixed with an adjuvant, is injected intramuscularly.
  2. Antigen presentation: Dendritic cells at the injection site engulf the vaccine antigen, break it into peptide fragments, and display them on their surface bound to HLA class II molecules. (This is why HLA typing matters — the patient's HLA molecules must be able to bind and present the specific vaccine peptide.)
  3. T cell activation: In the lymph node, dendritic cells present the antigen-HLA complex to CD4+ helper T cells whose receptors recognize that specific combination.
  4. B cell activation: B cells whose surface antibodies can bind the vaccine antigen are activated, with critical co-stimulatory help from the activated T cells.
  5. Germinal center reaction: In lymph node germinal centers, B cells undergo somatic hypermutation (random antibody gene mutations), affinity maturation (selection of the best binders), and class switching (IgM to IgG).
  6. Antibody production: Selected B cells become plasma cells that move to bone marrow and secrete high-affinity IgG antibodies into the bloodstream for months to years.
  7. Memory: Some B cells become long-lived memory cells, ready to rapidly reactivate upon booster doses.
  • Advantage: A single vaccination course generates long-lasting, self-sustaining antibody production. The body produces a polyclonal response (many different antibody species), providing broader coverage than a single monoclonal antibody.
  • Advantage: Cost-effective for chronic disease (boosters every 3-6 months vs monthly antibody infusions at $30,000-50,000/year)
  • Disadvantage: Response depends on the patient's immune system (HLA type, age, immune health)
  • Disadvantage: Takes weeks to months to generate antibodies; harder to stop if there's an adverse reaction
  • Examples for this patient: VacSYn (α-syn vaccine), AADvac1 (tau vaccine), UB-312 (α-syn vaccine)

Passive Immunotherapy (Monoclonal Antibodies)

Pre-made antibodies are infused directly into the bloodstream. They immediately begin binding to misfolded protein.

  • Advantage: Immediate effect — no waiting for immune response
  • Advantage: Consistent dosing — doesn't depend on patient's immune system
  • Disadvantage: Requires regular infusions (typically every 4 weeks)
  • Disadvantage: Expensive to manufacture
  • Examples for this patient: Prasinezumab (anti-α-syn), bepranemab (anti-tau)

How Antibodies Clear Misfolded Proteins

Once antibodies against alpha-synuclein or tau are circulating, they can clear pathological protein through several mechanisms:

  1. Peripheral sink hypothesis: Antibodies in the blood bind target protein in the blood and peripheral tissues, shifting the equilibrium. This draws the target protein out of the brain (down the concentration gradient), where it is cleared by the liver and kidneys. This mechanism doesn't require antibodies to enter the brain.
  2. Direct neutralization: The small fraction of antibodies that cross the BBB can bind to extracellular protein aggregates and seeds, preventing them from seeding further aggregation or being taken up by neighboring neurons — blocking cell-to-cell spread.
  3. Microglial phagocytosis: When antibodies bind to aggregates, their Fc region (the "tail" of the Y-shaped antibody) is recognized by Fc receptors on microglia (brain immune cells), which engulf and digest the antibody-aggregate complex. This is one of the most important clearance mechanisms.
  4. Prevention of cross-seeding: Critically for dual proteinopathy, antibodies that neutralize alpha-synuclein oligomers could prevent those oligomers from cross-seeding tau (and vice versa), breaking the amplification cycle at either point.

The Blood-Brain Barrier Problem

Antibodies are large molecules. The blood-brain barrier (BBB) — a tight layer of endothelial cells lining brain blood vessels, connected by tight junctions — blocks most antibodies from entering the brain. Only about 0.1% of circulating antibodies reach the brain (crossing via the FcRn receptor on endothelial cells). This is a major limitation for all immunotherapy in neurodegeneration — to get therapeutic brain levels, very high blood levels are needed.

Solutions being developed:

  • Brain-shuttled antibodies: Engineered with a second binding arm targeting the transferrin receptor (TfR) on brain endothelial cells. TfR undergoes constant transcytosis (shuttling iron into the brain), and the antibody hitchhikes across. Achieves 5-40x higher brain concentrations. (Tier 3 — preclinical, npj Parkinson's 2025)
  • Focused ultrasound BBB opening: Low-intensity focused ultrasound combined with IV microbubbles (tiny gas-filled spheres) causes the microbubbles to oscillate, mechanically loosening tight junctions for a window of several hours. During this window, antibodies pass into targeted brain regions at 10-100x higher concentrations. Shown safe in Phase 1 PD dementia trial. (Tier 2)
  • Intrathecal delivery: Injecting antibodies directly into the CSF via lumbar puncture, bypassing the BBB entirely. More invasive but guarantees CNS exposure. Used by some ASO programs.
  • Nasal delivery: The olfactory epithelium has a direct path to the brain via the olfactory nerve (bypasses the BBB). The anti-pT181 mucosal vaccine uses this approach.

17. Neuroprotection (GLP-1 Agonists)

GLP-1 receptor agonists (originally developed for diabetes) have shown neuroprotective effects. They activate neuronal survival pathways, reduce neuroinflammation, and promote neurotrophic factor release.

DrugEvidenceStatusActionability
Lixisenatide Tier 1 NEJM 2024: reduced motor disability progression in Phase 2 Positive Phase 2; further trials planned Available now as diabetes medication (off-label use). Discuss with neurologist.
Exenatide Tier 1 Lancet 2025: Phase 3 NEGATIVE Did not slow progression in Phase 3 Not recommended. But other GLP-1RAs may differ.
Next-gen GLP-1 agonists Tier 2 5 ongoing trials with newer agents Monitor. Class is not dead despite exenatide failure.
Why GLP-1 agonists matter for dual proteinopathy: Unlike immunotherapies that target one specific protein, GLP-1 agonists provide neuroprotection regardless of the protein pathology. They protect neurons from dying, regardless of whether the damage is driven by alpha-synuclein, tau, or both. This makes them particularly relevant for dual proteinopathy.

18. Emerging Approaches

Focused Ultrasound + Immunotherapy

MRI-guided focused ultrasound temporarily opens the blood-brain barrier, potentially allowing antibodies to reach brain tissue at much higher concentrations. Phase 1 demonstrated safety in PD dementia. Combining FUS with immunotherapy is the next frontier. Particularly relevant for dual proteinopathy — could deliver both α-syn and tau antibodies in the same session.

Brain-Shuttled Antibodies

Engineered antibodies with a transferrin receptor binding domain that crosses the BBB at 10-40x higher concentrations than standard antibodies. Published in npj Parkinson's Disease 2025. Could solve the BBB problem for both α-syn and tau antibodies.

Dendritic Cell Vaccines

Preclinical research shows dendritic cell vaccines can be dual-loaded with both alpha-synuclein AND tau antigens — potentially addressing both pathologies simultaneously with a single vaccine. This is still early-stage (Tier 3) but uniquely relevant for dual proteinopathy.
